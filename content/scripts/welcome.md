**HOST_B:** So I finally cloned your project last night. And I still want to hear you say it out loud. What is this?

**HOST_A:** A tutor. A specific person — a name from the culture, a way of talking, and a memory of exactly where you are. The coding agent you already use interviews you — which language, whose table you want to talk at, what you already hold — then writes everything downstream: the persona, a charter for your dialect, a starting curriculum, native voices, and a phone loop so the tutor reaches you first.

**HOST_B:** Okay. And what counts as progress? Because I have "finished" a lot of lesson trees.

**HOST_A:** One number. Of everything you understand, how much can you actually say when the moment comes? The system calls it the viability floor — the only score anywhere in it. Comprehension grows almost for free. Production doesn't. And it climbs from whatever you already hold — nobody starts at lesson one.

**HOST_B:** That's me and my grandmother's Spanish, honestly. I follow everything in her kitchen. I answer in English.

**HOST_A:** Then you already know the gap. Every session spends its ten or fifteen minutes on the saying side: the tutor hands you a situation — your uncle piles more food on your plate, the waiter is standing there — and wants the line back from you, in the language. Miss it, and it says it the natural way and moves on. The way family corrects you.

**HOST_B:** And on the days I don't show up? Because my calendar is where habits go to die.

**HOST_A:** The tutor reaches out — your phone gets one line, sometimes a voice. If the language you're after is your grandmother's Spanish, your lock screen sounds like this:

**ABUELA:** Mijo. ¿Ya comiste? Contéstame en español — one line, mijo, that's all I'm asking.

**HOST_B:** ...okay. That's unfair. That's her.

**HOST_A:** One line back, typed right into the notification. Ten seconds — a real repetition, graded, remembered, built on. And tell it you're slammed and it backs off. A quiet week is information, not a failure.

[Pause: 1 sec]

**HOST_B:** The podcast part. Explain the podcast part.

**HOST_A:** Whatever you struggled to say in the morning comes back that afternoon as a short episode — a scene built from your words, in native voices, on your private feed. Mouth in the morning, ear in the afternoon — that loop is the engine.

**HOST_B:** Every app claims "how people really talk," though. What's actually different here?

**HOST_A:** The tutor derives your language's real machinery, instead of shipping one curriculum with new vocabulary bolted on. Take Tamil — not a demo language: the worked example. A real Tamil tutor ran daily for months, and this repo is what was extracted from it.

**HOST_A:** Two things make Tamil a brutal proving ground. First, the drift between book and street isn't one gap — it's layers. There's a classical literary Tamil, centuries of poetry deep — what Shakespeare is to you. There's the formal written Tamil of the newspaper, the news desk, and the textbook. And under both there's the spoken language, clipping and welding words together until the written forms sound like a costume. The textbook hands you the middle layer. Show up with it, and you're reading the evening news at the kitchen table. This is Anna — it means elder brother — the tutor that problem produced:

**ANNA:** Your maama is at the door with sweets. The textbook taught you வாருங்கள். No aunty in Coimbatore has ever said வாருங்கள் at a door. You say வாங்க வாங்க — come, come, come in! Sollu. Say it back.

**HOST_B:** He slid between the two languages without blinking.

**HOST_A:** That's the weave — English carries the story, the language carries the line you need. Spanglish, Franglais, Thanglish — the way mixed households actually talk. The second thing is the strange one. An English verb travels light — go, went, gone — and the rest of the sentence carries everything else. A Tamil verb is built up. Endings stack onto the root, and every ending is a decision: when it happened. Who did it — one person, or the whole family. Whether it happened at all. How final you mean it. And a decision English has never once asked of you: how much respect the person in front of you is owed. A whole sentence's worth of meaning arrives packed inside a single word — and one slot in that word is where you stand. Tamil even keeps two words for "we" — one that includes the person you're talking to, and one that leaves them out.

**ANNA:** சொல்லு — say it — that's for the kid at the table. சொல்லுங்க for the elder at the head of it. Same word. The ending tells everyone where you stand.

**HOST_A:** English doesn't have that structure, so an English-first curriculum would never think to drill it. Nobody implemented a respect module. The interview asked which language; the agent derived the charter; the toggle fell out — and because it's load-bearing in Tamil, every repetition carries it.

**HOST_B:** And in a language I'd actually pick?

**HOST_A:** Different machinery surfaces. Turkish would hand you evidence — it keeps two past tenses, one for what you saw happen and one for what you only heard about, so hearsay wears a uniform. Japanese would hand you all the elegant ways to never say "you." Whatever your language encodes is what gets drilled, in context, from day one. That's the difference between a tutor and a curriculum.

[Pause: 1 sec]

**HOST_B:** What does it cost me, day to day?

**HOST_A:** Ten minutes of being wrong out loud, most days. There's no shortcut in it — the floor only moves when you say new things cold — but every one of those minutes lands on your gap, in your dialect, and the tutor comes to find you.

**HOST_B:** And when it corrects me in a way I hate?

**HOST_A:** Every rule it follows is a page of prose in the repo. Its constitution, its persona, your language's charter — plain files. Read them, edit them, and the tutor behaves differently tomorrow. Try filing that ticket with an app.

[Pause: 1 sec]

**HOST_B:** Last one. Why does this exist?

**HOST_A:** Because the first tutor worked. The Tamil one — built for exactly the gap you named: years of understanding a room, and answering it in English. Its whole spec was one sentence:

**ENGINEER:** இனிமே நான் பேசுவேன்.

**HOST_A:** "From now on, I will speak." Everything in the repo serves that sentence. And the project is named for the word the tutor says when he hands you a line and waits:

**ANNA:** சொல்லு.

**HOST_A:** Sollu. Say it. Short of a human tutor on retainer, this is the best coach you can get — it knows your language's actual machinery, your dialect, your people, and exactly where your floor is. And it shows up first. Clone it, and say: set up my tutor.
