**HOST_B:** So you know I've quit three language apps. Be honest with me. What is this one?

**HOST_A:** It's not an app. That's most of the answer, actually. There's no streak, no leaderboard, no owl guilting you at nine p.m. It's a small open repository — you clone it, open the coding agent you already use, and say: set up my tutor. It interviews you — your language, your people, where you actually are — and then it writes you a tutor.

**HOST_B:** Just like that.

**HOST_A:** The interview's the easy part. Then there's the plumbing — API keys, voices, hooking up your phone — and that takes as long as plumbing takes. It's honest about that too. The agent does the wiring; you hold the flashlight.

**HOST_B:** Okay. So it writes me a tutor.

**HOST_A:** A person, more or less. A name from the culture, a way of talking, and a memory of where you actually are — not lesson one. It was built by an engineer who married into a Tamil-speaking family. Years at that table. He understood a lot of what flew past him, and could say almost none of it back.

[Pause: 1 sec]

**HOST_B:** That's me and my grandmother's Spanish, honestly. I follow everything in her kitchen. I answer in English.

**HOST_A:** Then you're who it was built for. That gap — understanding plenty, saying nothing — is the one thing it works on. Most apps grow the understanding side. This one spends every day on the saying side. Ten, fifteen minutes: the tutor hands you a situation — your uncle piles more food on your plate, the waiter is standing there — and wants the line back from you, in the language. When you miss, it just says it the natural way and moves on. The way family corrects you. Not the way school did.

**HOST_B:** And when I don't find the time? Because that's how the other three died. Not on the hard days. On the busy ones.

**HOST_A:** That's the part I'd actually call different. You don't have to remember it exists. The tutor reaches out — your phone gets one line, sometimes a voice. And not a computer voice. If the language you're after is your grandmother's Spanish, what lands on your lock screen sounds like this:

**ABUELA:** Mijo. ¿Ya comiste? Contéstame en español — one line, mijo, that's all I'm asking.

**HOST_B:** ...okay. That's unfair. That's her.

**HOST_A:** One line back, typed right into the notification. Ten seconds. That counts — that's a whole rep. And if you tell it you're busy, it takes that as an answer and backs off. Its own instructions say a quiet week is information, not a failure.

[Pause: 1 sec]

**HOST_B:** And the podcast part? I saw there's a podcast part.

**HOST_A:** Whatever you struggled to say this morning, it turns into a short episode by the afternoon — a small scene built from exactly your words, in native voices, on your podcast feed. You strained to say it; now you hear it used, by people who sound like the people you want to talk to. That loop — from your mouth to your ear and back — is the whole engine.

**HOST_B:** Okay. Five years of Ontario school French. Is that in there somewhere, or do I start over?

**HOST_A:** You never start over. That's the first thing the interview does — it maps what you already hold. School French that's asleep. Kitchen Spanish. And it teaches the register people actually speak, not the one in the workbook. Here's a counter in Montréal:

**MONTREALER:** Pis, c'est-tu pour ici ou pour emporter? Tu veux-tu un reçu?

**MONTREALER:** Five years of school French, and nobody ever taught you *c'est-tu*. That is the French you will actually hear. This is what I mean by the register.

**HOST_A:** It derives that — the real spoken register of your dialect — for your language, on day one. And it writes it down where you can read it.

**HOST_B:** And if the gap between the book and the street is bigger than that?

**HOST_A:** Then take the language this was built on. Tamil. The written language and the spoken language have drifted so far apart they're nearly two languages — learn from a textbook and you'll sound like a newsreader at a kitchen table. The engineer's tutor is called Anna — it means elder brother — and Anna talks like this:

**ANNA:** Your maama is at the door with sweets. The textbook taught you வாருங்கள். No aunty in Coimbatore has ever said வாருங்கள் at a door. You say வாங்க வாங்க — come, come, come in! Sollu. Say it back.

**HOST_B:** He just slid between the two without blinking.

**HOST_A:** That's the weave. English carries the story; the language carries the one line you need — the way mixed households actually talk. Spanglish. Franglais. Thanglish. The tutor works out where that switch is natural for your language — in some it isn't, and it writes that rule down too — so from day one you're speaking the way the room speaks, at whatever size your vocabulary is that morning.

[Pause: 1 sec]

**HOST_B:** What's the catch?

**HOST_A:** It's honest work. Ten minutes most days, saying things out loud that you'll get wrong. There's no shortcut in it, and it won't tell you you're further along than you are — it tracks one number, and the number is just: of the words you understand, how many can you actually say when the moment comes. The other catch, if it is one — it's plain files. Every rule the tutor follows is a page you can read. If you don't like how it corrects you, you can edit its constitution.

**HOST_B:** Who is it for, then? Really.

**HOST_A:** One person at a time. Remember the engineer at the Tamil dinner table — years of being the quiet one in the room. The day he started, the mission statement was one sentence:

**ENGINEER:** இனிமே நான் பேசுவேன்.

**HOST_A:** "From now on, I'll speak." That was the whole spec. It's public because that problem isn't rare. Your table, your language, your level. That's it. That's the pitch.

**HOST_B:** That's barely a pitch.

**HOST_A:** I know. That's on purpose.

[Pause: 1 sec]
